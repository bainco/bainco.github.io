# Computational Thinking Across Disciplines
_Course Proposal_ - Upper Level Undergraduate Course (15 - 30 students) – 3 Class Periods (50 mins) each week

## Overarching Argument
As both a computer scientist and an educator, I feel that students often do not realize the extent to which their field of study is driven by computation. In addition, while you often hear about what computers can do, you don’t often hear what they can’t do. This course will provide an opportunity to students to not only get their feet wet in the computational realm by playing with software, programming languages, and other computational devices, but also provide them an opportunity to explore how and in what ways their own interests are explorable via computation.

# Course Description
In an increasingly computational world, we often find ourselves immersed in technology we don’t fully understand. While computational tools have proliferated nearly every discipline, learning how to uses these tools effectively is left as an exercise in independent learning. This class changes that–moving this new idea of computational thinking into a classroom setting where students get to pursue their own interests using the power of computation.

In this class, we re-examine the most important part of problem solving: the problem definition. Here we learn and implement a framework for identifying parts of a problem that computation can help us solve. From there, we survey computational tools, both universal and domain- specific, and learn to use and evaluate them based on your needs.

In addition, we will develop the skills necessary to communicate when, how, and why we use a computational tool to those who might not be familiar with it.

Throughout the course, you’ll pursue a group project in some domain of your choosing. The only restriction is that you must be able to use some sort of computational tool (it doesn’t have to be one we cover in class) as a primary means of exploration or analysis.

The main goal of the course is to show students that computation isn’t simply a workhorse but is instead a new method of inquiry and expression.

# Course Objectives
1. Develop the ability to break down a project into sub-problems that lend themselves to computational solutions
2. Understand how to find and evaluate computational tools for a given problem (and in a given domain)
3. Recognize computing tools as an additional means of expressing ideas
4. Demonstrate competence in being able to communicate your chosen computational
methods to a non-expert audience
5. Plan, design, and carry out a project in which computation is used a primary means of
discovery and understanding

# Assessments
Because the course is centered around empowering students with computational tools, most of the assignments focus on getting students discovering, using, and evaluating different tools. These differ from traditional computer science assignments in that they are almost exclusively driven by students’ interests.

For the software inspection exercises, I will be picking tools based on students’ fields and interests, in the spirit of responsive teaching (Bowers & Flinders, 1990). In addition, nearly all of the assignments allow students to pick something that is meaningful to them instead of being prescribed a certain tool or topic. My hope is that this creates a classroom environment where students see that their ideas, thoughts, and interests are an integral part of the class.

Finally, all of the assignments have a secondary focus on communication which aims to contextualize the skills that students learn in a world that requires not only computational thinkers but computational communicators and translators.

| Objectives | Assessments                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                                  |
|------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| 1, 2, 3, 4 | *2 short computational exploration plans* (2-3 pages double spaced) describing a problem of interest and how it might be approached from a computational perspective                                                                                                                           |
| 2, 3, 4    | *Weekly software inspection exercise* where groups of students have 15 minutes to analyze a piece of software and identify what it is good at, who its audience is, and what it’s not so good at (each week students are asked to identify candidates for this exercise from their own fields) |
| 1, 3, 5    | *Computational essay* (equivalent to 5 pages) that takes a topic of interest and explores it in a computational manner. These need to be readable by a layperson without sacrificing the core computational ideas that were leveraged                                                          |
| 5          | *Final project* where a group (3 to 4 students) pursues either creating some sort of computational tool or an in-depth investigation of some problem of interest using computation.                                                                                                            |
| 4          | *Presentation* _of another group’s project_. One week before the final, each group will receive another group’s project and for the final, will present the project to the class.                                                                                                                |

The key to these assignments is making them approachable and open-ended. Often, computer science is taught in a fashion that is disconnected to what students see in the real-world (Margolis, 2003). This class aims to allow students of all backgrounds and skill levels to engage with computation, instead of catering only to those who wish to study formal computer science, by focusing on real-world softwares, interests, and assignments.
