Winter Retreat

Created in December 12, 2023

2023   ·   rituals   pedagogy     ·   retreat-reflection  

Today’s retreat was centered on Picture a Professor. Now that I’ve decided on completing my Searle Fellows Project in the context of my COMP_SCI 110 course, I’m going to try to specifically reflect on some of the ideas from the retreat in that context.

COMP_SCI 110 is called “Intro to Computer Programming” and is a challenging course to teach because it’s:

  • the only course in the entire Computer Science Department which is majority non-major students
  • the only course CS course that specifically does not count toward a major or minor in CS
  • a large-format course (250 to 350 students per quarter)

While I do my best to present myself as a “guide” through the material towards a greater literacy, in a large-format course like this, it’s often hard to not only present myself that way…but to have students interpret my role in the classroom that way. In an effort to be as transparent as psosible, my syllabus is quite lengthy in this class – mainly to make sure that not only is there a “rule for everything” but that everything is written down for students to access without any need to inquire through me. That way, I’m purposefully removing myself from the loop. I don’t make judgement calls, I don’t stand as a barrier to anyone; instead, I’m only there to help you get past your learning roadblocks, while they syllabus is there to help you with timing, assessment, and flexibility if necessary.

Of course this causes its own issues in a course this size.

But back to the session itself. I found quite a bit of resonance in the idea of ritual as it’s an important part of my course design. I call it a course “rhythm” but it essentially encompasses the same ideas. In my large-format courses I follow a MWF course rhythm that looks like this:

  • Monday - Passive Lecture Day
  • Wednesday - Active Workshop (Tutorial) Day
  • Friday - Practice+ Day (Active Lecture with short activities)

I do this one because I feel that having a sense of “ritual” or “normality” in a class makes it more inviting. Secondly, this alternating between different modes of interaction (hopefully) makes it so that you can’t “sink into” any particular way of interacting with the course. I’ve never investigated the effect of this formally, but found that it kept people a little more consistently engaged (and perhaps equally importantly, helped prevent some burnout on my part – though it just shifts work from the weekday to the weekend for me).

One thing that I need to spend some more time thinking about in the context of this retreat is how do I show all of the work I do for accessibility in my courses in my teaching statement. It doesn’t really fit anywhere in my current statement, yet I’ve devoted quite a bit of effort to it so it should be featured somwhere.
