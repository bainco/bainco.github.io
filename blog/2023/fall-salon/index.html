Fall Teaching Salon

Created in October 12, 2023

2023   ·   equity   rigor     ·   salon-reflection  

Today’s workshop was a meditation on The Norton Guide to Equity-Minded Teaching. We discussed a number of equity-centered topics focused on identity and belonging–both in the classroom and in larger academic communities.

However, the topic I most resonated with in terms of my project is the idea of defining the concept of rigor in the classroom. Our group spent quite a bit of time discussing this idea because rigor in science classes is often interpreted in a specific way. In these classrooms, rigor is almost always defined in terms of not only what senior faculty (“the field”) think should be covered in a class but also the way in which that material should be covered. In STEM classrooms, this often translates to:

  • Exams that emphasize memorization
  • “Low” exam averages (or “curved” exam grades)
  • The concept of “weed-out” courses
  • The overall idea that “if you can’t handle this you can’t be here”

All of these ideas are at odds with the concept of COMP_SCI 110 (the course in which I’m working on my Searle Fellows project). I don’t wish to assign students arbitrarily low grades. It’s not a course that counts toward the computer science major (thus can’t be a gate keeper course). I do not think that memorization is a particularly important tool in CS (though note that I think that memorization and internalization are linked).

My definition of rigor (in the classroom) is very much different than this. Frankly, I don’t think that rigor is a necessary term when it comes to learning. Challenge is a better word. Though I acknowledge this comes from my own educational ethos, I believe that challenge is a necessary part of a course. We shouldn’t take courses to be told what we need to know. We should take courses to challenge ourselves to go beyond what we can currently do. This, for each person, looks different.

In 110, we’re asking you to learn a new way of thinking. After all, computer programming is simply the act of translating what’s in a human’s head into terms a computer can understand. This, in and of itself, is a rigorous idea. To form a new literacy is an incredibly deep (not to mention authentic) task. Now, can we expect someone to become an expert in 10 weeks? No – but is expertise the standard? Are all the students in this course seeking expertise? Or are they simply seeking exposure?

I don’t have any answers here, but I do think at least the following:

  • Challenge isn’t anathema to fairness
  • Rigor can be accomplished without exclusion
  • Grades alone are not indicative of rigor
  • Course design can allow for pursuits at varying levels of engagement (rigor) for wider audiences
